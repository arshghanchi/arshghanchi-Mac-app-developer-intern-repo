### 1. How a Kanban Board Works
A Kanban board visualises the flow of work.  
Tasks move from left to right, representing progress from **planned** â†’ **in progress** â†’ **completed**.

It helps teams:
- See what everyone is working on  
- Identify bottlenecks  
- Maintain a steady workflow  
- Improve collaboration and transparency

### 2. What the Columns Represent
Typical Kanban columns include:

- **Backlog** â€“ Ideas, tasks, or requests waiting to be prioritised  
- **Ready / To Do** â€“ Tasks prepared with enough information to start  
- **In Progress** â€“ Actively being worked on  
- **Review / Testing / QA** â€“ Work awaiting verification or testing  
- **Blocked** â€“ Tasks that cannot progress due to dependencies or issues  
- **Done** â€“ Fully completed and accepted  

These stages may vary slightly depending on the team's workflow.

---

### 3. How Tasks Move Through the Board
- The developer (or assignee) pulls tasks from **To Do** â†’ **In Progress** as they start work  
- Once completed, the developer moves items to **Review/Testing**  
- QA or another teammate verifies quality and moves it forward  
- Blocked tasks are placed in **Blocked** with a note explaining the issue  
- The assignee is responsible for updating task status, ensuring accurate visibility for the whole team  

Kanban relies on **continuous updates** so the board always reflects the real state of work.

---

### 4. Benefits of Limiting WIP (Work in Progress)
WIP limits prevent multitasking overload and improve focus. Benefits include:
- Faster task completion  
- Reduced context switching  
- Fewer bottlenecks  
- Clearer priorities  
- More predictable workflow  
- Higher quality output  

Teams are encouraged to finish existing tasks before starting new ones.
